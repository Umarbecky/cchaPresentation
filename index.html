Headline insight

Adoption opportunity

This is based on a small sample of 10 respondents, so the data should be treated as directional user feedback and lessons learned rather than a statistical survey.

01 · Survey snapshot

Response overview

The survey captured structured ratings and open-text feedback across the Phase 1 user experience, including go-live, training, confidence, usability, Agile tools, delivery lessons and Phase 2 improvements.

How to read the results

  • Small sample: 10 responses means each response represents 10 percentage points when all respondents answered.
  • Clear qualitative signal: training, system navigation, team capacity, reporting, data and planning appear repeatedly across open-text responses.
  • Action-focused: the strongest value is in using the feedback to improve Phase 2 readiness, onboarding and adoption.

02 · Overall experience

Phase 1 created a positive foundation, with clear improvement areas

Most respondents rated the overall experience as Good or Very Good, while the open-text feedback shows that training, readiness, communication and support need to be strengthened before Phase 2.

60%

rated the overall experience as Good or Very Good

Phase 1 created a positive foundation, with 6 out of 10 respondents rating the overall experience as Good or Very Good. However, the remaining responses and open-text comments highlight important opportunities to improve user readiness, training, communication and post-go-live support.

“A more structured and transparent approach to readiness would have improved the go-live experience.”

03 · Confidence and usability

Users are gaining confidence, but usability remains uneven

Confidence feedback is broadly positive, with 6 out of 9 respondents feeling confident or mostly confident using the system. Usability is more mixed, with a large neutral group and 3 respondents rating the system as difficult or very difficult to use.

67% felt confident or mostly confident
3 users still showed some confidence risk
3 rated usability as difficult or very difficult

04 · Training and support

Training is the strongest recurring theme

Feedback repeatedly calls for practical, team-specific, scenario-led training, refresher support and clearer guidance on how to complete day-to-day tasks in Salesforce.

78% rated training as not effective or slightly effective

6/8 support-now responses referenced training or guidance

06 · Pain points

The barriers are practical, not abstract

The most common concerns relate to training, navigation, information visibility, data, testing, reporting, internal workload and consistency of use across teams.

07 · Delivery lessons

Delivery needs stronger readiness control

Users asked for more structured planning, clearer ownership, better session pacing, stronger requirements logging and enough protected time for testing and re-testing.

Readiness

Use go-live readiness criteria covering training, testing, data, support routes, communications and user access.

Ownership

Agree a simple RACI so users know who owns decisions, testing, data queries, training and sign-off.

Workload

Work with Heads of Service upfront to protect time for workshops, testing, training and re-testing.

Requirements

Keep one structured requirements and test basis to reduce duplicated spreadsheets, missed decisions and rework.

Documentation

Create field mapping, data dictionary, reporting guidance, quick reference guides and known-issue logs.

Scenario testing

Use real cases and team-specific scenarios, particularly where work is complex or varies significantly.

08 · Agile tools

Agile tools were useful where understood, but onboarding gaps affected adoption

Feedback shows that Jira, Confluence and Agile ways of working were helpful for users who had the right context and early exposure. However, several end users were not onboarded in time or missed key training, which created a mixed experience across teams.

Mixed

smooth for some users, challenging for others

The issue was not necessarily the Agile tools themselves, but the timing and consistency of onboarding. Phase 2 should include a short “ways of working” session covering Jira, Confluence, testing cycles, re-testing, decision logs, feedback routes and user responsibilities.

“Useful to see progress.”

11 · Recommendations

What should happen next

The Phase 1 feedback should now be treated as a practical readiness checklist for Phase 2.

1

Run team-specific training

Use real scenarios from each team rather than generic system demonstrations.

2

Introduce a readiness gate

Do not move to go-live until training, testing, data, access and support readiness are confirmed.

3

Set up a clear hypercare model

Define where users go for support, who triages issues and how feedback is tracked.

4

Improve project communication

Use one agreed route for updates, decisions, actions, FAQs and known issues.

5

Explain Agile ways of working

Give users a simple introduction to Jira, Confluence, UAT, re-testing and acceptance.

6

Strengthen documentation

Create field mapping, data dictionary, reporting guidance and quick reference guides.

12 · Closing message

Phase 1 created the foundation. Phase 2 needs to build consistency and confidence.

The feedback shows that users can see the potential in Salesforce, but adoption will depend on practical training, clearer delivery controls, better onboarding, stronger documentation and more realistic planning around business workloads.
